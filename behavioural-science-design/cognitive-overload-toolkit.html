Cognitive Overload Toolkit

Our toolkit for coping with cognitive overload is Cowry’s SLICE framework - Saliency, Layout, Imagery, Colour and End Goal. This will give you the tools to make experiences more psychologically appealing.

Saliency

Perceptual fluency is the ease with which people process information.

The easier it is to process something, the more likely we are to do a task and the more likely we are to like the objects/information involved.

Therefore, text that is smaller, has low contrast and is in a generally less readable font will have a negative impact on the reader.

To increase perceptual fluency, make sure that text:

  • Is large enough to read with ease
  • Has high contrast against the background
  • Is in a simple font (such as Avenir)

Layout

The main purpose of your communication is to engage the reader with the required tasks they need to complete, therefore the visual hierarchy of the page is important.

Things to consider when creating a visual hierarchy:

  • People focus on what is highest and what is largest on the page
  • People read from left to right
  • Too much on the page can cause Cognitive Overload
  • Colour, size, contrast and many more variables can be used to increase saliency

Imagery

Visual images are not only excellent for communicating but also rapidly affect us psychologically and physiologically. This is because pictures are more perceptually distinct than text. We are much quicker to recognise what a picture is conveying, than what text is saying.

Additionally, the types of decisions we make are dependent upon our state of emotional arousal. If we see an item that we associate with love, sympathy or generosity, we are more likely to respond favourably. Generating a positive mood is a powerful tool to encourage optimistic behaviour.

Negative imagery such as exclamation marks and warning symbols could elicit feelings of punishment. Instead, positive images should be used to reinforce behaviour.

Colour

Colour can be used to influence people’s mental performance, cognitive performance and emotional state.

These effects are highly dependent upon context. For example, in a restaurant environment, red has been shown to increase appetite. However, red is also associated with failure and danger and in an achievement context can undermine performance on challenging tasks that require mental manipulation and flexibility.

There are two main categories of colours: warm colours (red, yellow, orange) and cold colours (blue, green, purple.)

Some experiments have yielded evidence suggesting that viewing blue or green may be particularly beneficial for creative performance.

  • Blue colours convey sophistication, competence, high-quality, corporate or reliability
  • Green colours convey good taste, envy, eco-friendly, health or low-quality
  • Red colours convey lust, speed, excitement, power, love or anger

End-Goal

Make the call to action in email, app or web clear by using clearly defined buttons and adhering to the following principles:

  • Purpose: make it clear that the button should be clicked on by adding depth and shadow. Research has shown that effective affordance cues such as changing the shape of the lid slot on office bins increased compliance of waste disposal by 40%
  • Ease: utilise Fitt’s law by increasing the size and salience of the button to increase click rate.
  • Find: ensure colour contrast of the target button to increase click rate. Research using a visual search task demonstrated that when there was a subtle colour difference between the target and the distracting stimuli, identification of the target was significantly lower than when the colour contrast was increased.

An example of End-Goal is displaying a progress bar that gives the user an estimate of how long the task will take to complete, which manages their expectation prospects.

Furthermore, partially filling the progress bar will increase the user’s efforts to complete due to the goal-gradient effect. This is where, as we reach a goal, our efforts towards that goal increase.
