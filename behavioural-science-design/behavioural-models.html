Behavioural Models

A summary of the key behavioural models or theories that can be used to identify customer behaviours.

Models of behaviour help us to understand specific behaviours, by identifying the underlying factors, which influence them.

Understanding Behavioral Models

A model is a description of key concepts/factors (predictive) and a theory attempts to give a full picture (explanatory).

A behavioural model is a representation of key factors or constructs in a system, which is used to describe relevant characteristics, make predictions and develop interventions.

A theory explains a set of phenomena, which is used to understand the phenomena, guide observation and develop interventions.

The value of having a model

Advantages

  • Define the factors at play in subconscious decision making
  • Show the relative importance of factors
  • Show the interaction between factors
  • Identify where to intervene

Disadvantages

  • Can’t use models as a perfect description of behaviour
  • Models don’t take into consideration all the factors
  • Models have limitations in telling you how to intervene

Behavioural Models and Theories

Trans-Theoretical Model

An integrative, biopsychosocial model to conceptualise the process of intentional behaviour change.

Find out more

COM-B

The COM-B model is a behaviour change framework that proposes three necessary components for any behaviour to occur.

Find out more

Theory of Planned Behaviour

A psychological theory linking beliefs to behaviour, suggesting that attitude, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control shape intentions.

Find out more

The Hook Model

The hook model describes how digital products can retain customers by getting them to form habits. Customers repeatedly pass through the four stages: trigger, action, variable reward, and investment.
