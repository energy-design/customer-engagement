The Hook Model

The Hook Model is a four-phase process that businesses can use to create products or services used habitually by customers. The goal is to result in voluntary, high-frequency engagement.

At its core, the Hook Model is about creating a customer habit. It also seeks to connect a customer’s problem to a company’s solution with enough frequency to make the engagement an ongoing practice.

How does it work?

A concept introduced by Nir Eyal to describe how digital products can retain customers by getting them to form habits. Customers repeatedly pass through the four stages of the “hook”: trigger, action, variable reward, and investment.

What distinguishes the Hook Model from a plain vanilla feedback loop is the hook’s ability to create a craving. Feedback loops are all around us, but predictable ones don’t create desire. The unsurprising response of your fridge light turning on when you open the door doesn’t drive you to keep opening it again and again. However, add some variability to the mix and voila, intrigue is created.

Trigger

  • A trigger is the actuator of behaviour — the spark plug in the engine. Triggers come in two types: external and internal
  • Habit-forming products start by alerting users with external triggers like an email, a website link, or the app icon on a phone.

Action

  • Following the trigger comes the action: the behaviour done in anticipation of a reward. This can be a single easy action, or a deeper task.

Variable Reward

Once you’ve completed the action, there’s a feeling of anticipation before the Reward. These can be:

  • Rewards of the Tribe: looking for a sense of connectedness and social rewards
  • Rewards of the Hunt: Putting in effort into the search, finding new information of discovering new features
  • Rewards of the Self: Searching for intrinsic rewards such as mastery of a skill, feeling a sense of completeness or building personal capability.

Investment

  • The last phase of the Hook Model is where the user does a bit of work. The investment phase increases the odds that the user will make another pass through the hook cycle in the future
  • The investment occurs when the user puts something into the product or service such as time, data, effort, social capital or money
  • However, the investment phase isn’t about users opening up their wallets and moving on with their day. Rather, the investment implies an action that improves the service for the next go-around.

The value of using this model

Strengths

  • Provides a simple model for habit formation, and is particularly useful for products such as mobile applications
  • Many businesses rely on building and maintaining habits, so can be valuable for a number of client projects

Limitations

  • The model is not a validated model of behaviour change because it was designed by a marketer, not a psychologist
  • Because it is not validated, there is limited experimental evidence to support the model and its application
