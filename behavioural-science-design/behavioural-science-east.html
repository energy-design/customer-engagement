The EAST Framework

The EAST Framework is a tool to support the use of Behavioural Science nudges. Designed by The Behavioural Insights Team: The Government’s “Nudge Unit”. The EAST Framework helps to encourage, or Nudge, desired behaviours by making them Easy, Attractive, Social, and Timely. 

Make it Easy

  • Use the power of defaults – We like to go with the default option, as this is the easy choice. If you make an option the default, it makes it more likely that people will adopt it
  • Lessen the ’hassle factor’ of joining a service – People are often put off actions due to the effort needed to carry them out. Reducing the effort involved in a task can lead to increased uptake
  • Simplify messages – When we hear a clear message, we are more likely to respond. Often, response rates are increased if a complex goal is broken down into simpler, easier actions.

Make it Attractive

  • Attract attention – People are more likely to do something if we have our attention drawn towards it. This could be achieved through the use of images, colour, and personalisation
  • Design rewards and sanctions for maximum return on investment – Using alternative reward designs such as lotteries is a cheaper and often more effective way of incentivising behaviour.

Make it Social

  • Make it clear that most people behave in the desired manner – When we describe what most people do when faced with a certain situation, we will encourage others to copy them. In the same way, by emphasising a highly prevalent problem behaviour, we may inadvertently reinforce it
  • Utilise the power of social networks – People have a network of social relationships which shape their actions. As a result, behaviours can spread peer to peer in some cases
  • Get people to make a commitment to others – Commitment devices can tie us into doing something in advance, and making these commitments social is often crucial.

Make it Timely

  • Give people prompts when they are more likely to be receptive – When we make the same offer at different times, there can be vastly different levels of success. When our habits are already disrupted (such as when we move house), behaviour is usually easier to change
  • Think about the initial costs and benefits – People are typically more influenced when a cost or benefit occurs immediately, rather than later. Given that these initial costs and benefits have so much influence, policy makers should consider how to adjust them
  • Support how people plan their response to events – As we have seen in Module 1, there is a large gap between our intentions and actual behaviour. When we help people to identify the barriers to their action and create a plan to overcome them, they are more likely to be successful.
